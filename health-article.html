CMS Finalizes 3.8% Hospital Payment Rate Increase Amid Record Workforce Cost Pressures

The FY2027 Inpatient Prospective Payment System rule adds $4.2 billion to hospital reimbursements, but hospital groups say labor costs are rising three times faster — reigniting the perennial gap between Medicare rates and the actual cost of care.

Maya Kim MAY 27, 2026 · 9 min read

Key Takeaways

  • The 3.8% IPPS rate increase translates to $4.2B in additional Medicare payments across roughly 3,300 acute care hospitals.
  • Hospital labor costs grew 11.4% in FY2026, meaning the rate increase covers less than one-third of cost inflation for most systems.
  • New health equity reporting requirements take effect October 1, 2026, adding compliance obligations tied to 0.5% of payment adjustments.
  • Rural hospitals receive a separate 3.1% add-on adjustment, the largest rural-specific supplement since 2009.

The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services finalized its FY2027 Inpatient Prospective Payment System rule on Tuesday, confirming a 3.8% base rate increase that will add approximately $4.2 billion in Medicare reimbursements to acute care hospitals beginning October 1. The figure, which applies to roughly 3,300 hospitals, was slightly above the proposed 3.6% from April's preliminary rule — a concession CMS attributed to updated hospital market basket data showing higher-than-forecast input cost growth in the first quarter of 2026.

The reaction from hospital groups was muted. The American Hospital Association called the increase "a step in the right direction that falls well short of what is needed," noting that labor expenses — which comprise 55–65% of hospital operating costs — grew 11.4% in FY2026 according to the AHA's own index. That means the 3.8% payment increase covers roughly one-third of cost inflation for the median hospital, continuing a multi-year trend of reimbursement rates lagging behind actual operating expenses. Margins at non-profit health systems have been under pressure since 2022, with the median operating margin sitting at 1.8% through Q1 2026 according to Kaufman Hall data.

"We are being asked to absorb a structural workforce cost increase with a payment update that was designed for a different labor market. The math doesn't work for a growing number of our members."

Health Equity Reporting: New Compliance Requirements

Buried in the 1,700-page final rule is a new health equity reporting framework that will tie 0.5% of payment adjustments to hospitals' ability to collect and report social determinants of health data — including housing status, food security, and transportation access — for Medicare beneficiaries. Hospitals that fail to meet reporting thresholds face a proportional payment reduction. The requirement phases in over three years, with initial data collection obligations beginning with admissions on or after October 1, 2026.

Hospital IT vendors are already fielding inquiries about EHR workflow changes needed to capture the new data fields. The requirement will likely require custom build work at most institutions, as existing social determinants screening workflows were designed for clinical decision support rather than administrative reporting. CMS estimates compliance costs at approximately $47 million industry-wide, though health system CFOs contacted for this article put their internal estimates significantly higher — ranging from $2M to $18M per system depending on existing infrastructure. Watch Q4 2026 earnings calls at HCA, CommonSpirit, and Tenet for early signals on how the cost burden is being absorbed.
