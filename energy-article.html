Grid-Scale Battery Storage Hits Cost Parity With Natural Gas Peakers for the First Time

A BloombergNEF analysis confirms that 4-hour lithium-iron-phosphate battery systems now match the levelized cost of natural gas peaking plants in 14 US states — a threshold that analysts have said would fundamentally reshape utility capacity planning for decades.

Daniel Park MAY 27, 2026 · 8 min read

Key Takeaways

  • 4-hour LFP battery systems now carry a levelized cost of $89–$112/MWh in 14 states, matching or beating new gas peaker plants at current gas prices.
  • The crossover is driven by a 34% drop in LFP cell prices since 2023, primarily from expanded Chinese and US domestic manufacturing capacity.
  • Three major utilities — Duke Energy, PacifiCorp, and Xcel Energy — have already canceled planned gas peaker projects in favor of battery storage alternatives.
  • FERC interconnection queue reform is the single largest remaining bottleneck: 41 GW of storage projects are stalled in multi-year queue backlogs.

For the first time since lithium-ion batteries entered the grid-scale market, a rigorous independent analysis has confirmed that battery energy storage systems are cost-competitive with natural gas peaking plants without relying on subsidies or favorable market structures. The BloombergNEF Q2 2026 Energy Storage Market Outlook, released Tuesday, places the levelized cost of a 4-hour lithium-iron-phosphate system at $89–$112 per megawatt-hour across 14 US states — a range that overlaps with or undercuts new natural gas peaker plants at Henry Hub prices above $3.50/MMBtu. Gas currently trades at $4.12/MMBtu.

The crossover is the product of sustained LFP cell price declines — down 34% since Q1 2023 — driven by capacity expansions at Chinese manufacturers and the first wave of US domestic LFP cell plants coming online following DOE loan guarantees issued in 2024 and 2025. BloombergNEF notes that the cost parity threshold is not uniform: in states with high interconnection costs, challenging permitting timelines, or lower insolation that reduces solar-plus-storage economics, gas peakers remain cheaper on a pure LCOE basis. But the 14 states where parity has been achieved include California, Texas, Florida, and New York — together representing approximately 41% of US peak electricity demand.

"We modeled this crossover happening in 2028 at the earliest. The LFP price trajectory has been faster than almost every forecast, and the implications for utility IRP cycles are profound."

Utility Planning Cycles Begin to Shift

Three major investor-owned utilities have already responded to the cost parity milestone with concrete project cancellations. Duke Energy withdrew permit applications for two natural gas peaker plants in the Carolinas totaling 740 MW, redirecting the capital to a battery storage solicitation. PacifiCorp canceled a planned 300 MW gas facility in Wyoming, citing updated IRP modeling that showed storage achieving lower ratepayer costs over the 20-year planning horizon. Xcel Energy, which already has one of the industry's highest renewable penetration rates, announced it will not seek any new gas generation in its next integrated resource plan.

The interconnection queue remains the primary bottleneck to translating this economic signal into deployed capacity. FERC's Order 2023-A interconnection reform, which took effect in February, has begun to streamline the cluster study process — but 41 GW of battery storage projects are currently caught in pre-reform queue backlogs that will take 18–36 months to resolve under the new rules. Developer confidence, measured by monthly queue withdrawal rates, will be the most reliable leading indicator of how quickly the pipeline converts to actual construction. FERC interconnection queue withdrawal rates for storage projects dropped from 38% in Q4 2025 to 24% in Q1 2026, suggesting improving developer confidence — but the projects that survive the queue still face an average 4.1-year timeline from application to commercial operation.
